Calibration

What this didn't solve

Three honest gaps the work surfaced but did not close. They are not pivot moments — they describe the current state of low-code AI generation, observed from the inside.

Relational integrity in AI-generated low-code

AI can already produce schemas and field definitions reliably. What it does not yet do is encode the relational importance between tables — which foreign key matters for which workflow, which lookup carries which authority, which form blocks which downstream step. The Lego blocks exist; the architectural intent that connects them does not.

Honest calibration in AI builders

Every AI surface ends with "verify this — AI can make mistakes." That disclaimer is itself an admission the underlying problem is unsolved. The real question is whether the AI can flag, in advance, where its own confidence is not aligned with reality. HRC's projection-residuals (Phase 4) are a candidate mechanism. Nothing in the IDE yet uses them.

Dashboards from scratch

ForgeDS's .ds generator can produce a working schema. It cannot yet produce a working dashboard, which is often the crux of why a customer wanted the app in the first place. A parallel case study using Zia AI to build the same expense app produced solid schema scaffolding but missed cross-form linking, workflow logic, and permission hierarchy — the same gap from the other side of the same coin.

Low-code is the natural building material for AI app generation: pattern-based, modular, declarative. With reliable relational encoding and honest confidence calibration, end-to-end AI-built Zoho Creator apps become plausible inside the next year. The foundational building blocks are already in place. What's missing is the engineering of relational strength — and an AI honest enough to say where it isn't sure.

For the Both& assessment team

The five evaluation criteria

This showcase is structured to answer the five evaluation criteria identified in the original assessment analysis.

  • 01

    Can you analyse a process critically?

    Phase 1 found 7 flaws in the original flowchart before writing code.

  • 02

    Do you understand the business context?

    GL mapping, King IV governance, SARS compliance, delegation-of-authority matrices — the system was designed around why expense controls exist, not just how they work.

  • 03

    Can you learn a new tool under pressure?

    Zoho Creator was self-taught for the assessment. The response was to build a development engine for it.

  • 04

    Can you build a working prototype?

    Two shipped applications — expense + chargeback — both with .ds files ready for one-click Zoho import.

  • 05

    Can you communicate what you built and why?

    Every design decision is documented with rationale. Every compliance control traces to a specific King IV principle or SARS provision.

Beyond the assessment

  • Abstraction instinct. Seeing reusable patterns and extracting them into tools (ForgeDS) rather than copy-pasting between projects.
  • Domain transfer. Proving the tools work on a different problem (chargebacks) validates the abstraction was real, not accidental.
  • Depth over breadth. Going from "build a form" to "formalise the mathematical invariants that would guarantee an AI-generated form is correct" — each layer justified by a concrete problem hit one phase earlier, not by theoretical ambition.
  • Ship velocity, with calibration. 2 products shipped end-to-end (Phases 1 and 3). 3 explorations functional in scope but not yet complete (Phases 2, 5, 6). 1 research framework with a sealed core theorem (Phase 4). 133+ commits, 3 languages, 18 days. Every artifact public and auditable; honest about what's done and what isn't.
